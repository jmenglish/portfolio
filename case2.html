Prism Space


CASE STUDY
Storage and Organizational Service

“Light doesn’t need a medium to travel through. It can travel through the vacuum of space.”

Prism Space is a secure Cloud Storage and Organization site that helps minimize the chaos of information by providing a unique space for users to easily store content and share memories to connect with family and friends.



MY ROLE:  RESEARCH | STRATEGY | DESIGN | USER TESTING
Problem

Users are in need of a Cloud Storage and Organization service to store personal items and memories freeing up space and decluttering their devices due to the ever-evolving volume of information we accumulate in our day-to-day lives.




Solution

Prism Space simplifies the process of upload and storing information and makes sharing content social and engaging. Users can upload documents and photos to share with other users privately or on social media platforms.



Discovery

Competitive Analysis

The following competitors were studied for this project: Google Drive, EverNote, and Dropbox. The research was performed with a user survey sent out to contacts via social media outlets and Reddit. The focus of the survey was to get an understanding of users current behaviors, users preferences, and note any missing features that would be an asset to participants.

SWOT Analysis


Strengths

These competitors are excellent at simplifying the data storage process, allowing users to create various forms of information, and collaborating easily with others.

Opportunities

Identify key content users want to store and create a product that simplifies the process of storing, organizing, and sharing important items. Create a user-friendly mobile app that does not require additional tools to utilize all of its features.

Weaknesses

Google Drive requires multiple apps for each content format (ex: word, spreadsheets, etc.) which is not user-friendly for creating and organizing information on a mobile app.

Threats

There are many big name competitors in the market providing this service for years now. We would have a learning curve to catch-up, learn what they are doing well, and what we can do better.

Survey Summary

The findings show that participants use competitor services to store personal information such as documents, research and notes. Creating and collaborating come second to users. It is clear that participants are most interested in storing documents and pictures for safekeeping and to share with others. They want the security of saving memorable items and the social aspect of sharing them. I suspect that saving information off of their devices to increase storage space and keep information secure is the valuable end goal.

Wireframes to Mockups

The wireframes went through many iterations to determine the content to include and in what format that would best serve the user. The iterations underwent preference tests to narrow down the most functional options. The feedback gathered from the Wireframe User Testing was used to improve on and drive the direction of the high-fidelity designs. In this round of iterations, improvements to areas that were inconsistent or did not function well for users were addressed.

HomePage

The homepage included two versions that were tested against each other to find the most user friendly experience. In the first version, all of the content was included in a one-page layout to reduce user scrolling, provide company info, product features within view with plan options up front. In the second version, the focus was solely on the product's features and testimonials with additional service information moved to a separate page. The preference test showed that a majority of participants preferred a one-page layout regardless of extra scrolling. They wanted to see all content in one location which would minimize clicking, highlight the features, and introduce pricing. With this feedback, the homepage was updated to focus on product features, customer testimonials, and plan details.

Dashboard

There were many versions of the dashboard to narrow down how to display the features appropriately. User testing was utilized to find the optimal layout for users to easily access items for sharing or collaborating. A majority of participants stated that all information in view on the dashboard would simplify navigation making it more accessible by reducing unnecessary steps. Also, it would be easier to organize and find all content within view. These limitations were removed changing the folder structure to give users control of organizing content, naming their content, and by placing all features (upload, create, share, and collaborate) in the immediate view. The design was modified to group actions together in an ellipsis drop-down menu eliminating separate pages.

User Testing

Testing Approach

There were two types of testing performed for this case study: Recorded User testing and UsabilityHub preference tests. User testing for this product was performed for desktop only of both the home page and dashboard of this product. In hindsight, testing should have included mobile because the participants on the survey frequently used mobile apps (such as Google Drive).


Remote/In-person Testing

With the in-person/remote user testing, the participants completed three tasks: sign up for an account, add a piece of content, and organize a form of content to the dashboard. They were encouraged to speak their thoughts out loud on the experience of navigating through each page. With this approach to testing, I could observe the exact areas of the test participants struggled with and ask prompting questions to collect data.

Perference Testing

For the Preference Test, participants voted on UsabilityHub for their preferred design. User insight was gathered on the content layout, features, or design choices for both the homepage and dashboard. The results were very helpful in guiding the evolution of wireframes and in finalizing design choices. For example, I started to question the original layout of the homepage and created a second option to include a more creative design with the images and content. A preference test was used to gather feedback on which UI experience user's preferred. The results showed that a large majority of participants enjoyed the original layout because it was simple and more natural to read the information. In the end, my doubts about the initial homepage design was discredited by the participant's approval. Lesson learned!

Conclusion

Lessons Learned

In the beginning of this project, I made the assumption that users would want a tool mainly to create and collaborate on their business and personal ideas. The survey results proved my theory to be incorrect and change the direction of this project. Also, I overlooked the amount of users that preferred the mobile app of competitors over web-based services. Testing was focused on web-based designs but mobile should have been included as well.
